Course Conclusion\(^{(*)}\)

These notes have developed Model Predictive Control from first principles through to advanced topics, building a complete toolkit for constrained control design. This chapter summarises the arc of the material and highlights the connections between the chapters.

What We Covered

The course began with the foundations: Chapter 1 introduced MPC as optimisation-based control with explicit constraint handling, contrasting it with classical PID and LQR. Chapters 2–4 then developed the mathematical prerequisites—quadratic and linear programming (Chapter 2), continuous-to-discrete conversion (Chapter 3), and controllability/observability (Chapter 4)—that underpin every MPC formulation.

Chapter 5 derived the Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) as the unconstrained baseline, establishing the Riccati equation and showing how its solution provides the terminal cost \(P\) used in MPC. Chapter 6 introduced state estimation via the Kalman filter, completing the observer side of the separation principle.

The core of the course is Chapter 7, which presented constrained linear MPC: the condensed QP formulation, the receding horizon algorithm, and the rigorous stability theory based on terminal cost and terminal set constraints. Chapter 8 extended this to practical architectures—offset-free tracking with augmented disturbance models and target calculators, and economic MPC with time-varying price signals—demonstrated on applications including quadrotor control and building energy management. Chapter 9 implemented these ideas in a full closed-loop simulation combining plant model, Kalman observer, target calculator, and economic MPC controller.

The supplementary chapters covered three frontiers of modern MPC:

  • Tube MPC (Chapter on Robust MPC) developed the theory of robust constraint satisfaction under bounded disturbances, including minimal robust positively invariant (mRPI) sets, Pontryagin constraint tightening, and the tube control law \(u_k = v_0^\star - K(x_k - z_0^\star)\). The key insight is that robustness is achieved at no additional online computational cost—the extra work is entirely offline.

  • Nonlinear MPC (Chapter on NMPC) treated direct transcription methods (single shooting, multiple shooting, collocation), the CasADi/IPOPT workflow for general NLP-based MPC, and successive convexification (SCvx/GuSTO) for real-time nonlinear trajectory optimisation. The powered rocket landing and nonholonomic robot tracking applications demonstrated these methods on challenging engineering problems.

  • Learning-Based MPC (Chapter on MPC with Learning) presented four data-driven paradigms: Gaussian Process MPC for uncertainty-aware control with probabilistic constraint tightening, Koopman MPC for lifting nonlinear dynamics into linear prediction models, DeePC for model-free predictive control via Willems' Fundamental Lemma and Hankel matrices, and Learning MPC (LMPC) for iterative cost improvement using safe sets and data-driven terminal costs. The chapter also covered neural network dynamics models and the connections between MPC and reinforcement learning.

The Unifying Theme

Despite the breadth of topics, a single idea runs through every chapter: MPC is optimisation-based control with constraints. Whether the model is linear or nonlinear, known or learned from data, deterministic or uncertain, the controller at each time step solves an optimisation problem that encodes performance objectives and physical limits. The differences between MPC variants lie in how that optimisation problem is constructed and solved:

  • Linear MPC (Chapter 7) solves a QP.

  • Tube MPC tightens the QP constraints offline and adds a feedback correction online.

  • Nonlinear MPC solves an NLP, possibly via sequential convexification to exploit QP/SOCP structure.

  • GP-MPC and Koopman MPC learn a model and then solve a (modified) QP.

  • DeePC bypasses the model entirely, replacing it with data matrices in the optimisation.

Real-Time Implementation and Embedded MPC

Throughout these notes, we used solvers such as MATLAB's quadprog, YALMIP, scipy's L-BFGS-B, and CasADi/IPOPT. These are sufficient for simulation and prototyping but may be insufficient for millisecond-level control on microcontrollers or DSPs. Deploying MPC on embedded hardware requires specialised techniques:

  • Explicit MPC (Multiparametric Programming) : For small systems, the solution can be pre-calculated offline as a piecewise affine function of the state. On the chip, the "optimisation" reduces to if-else checks to find the correct polyhedral region, requiring microseconds to execute.

  • Code Generation: Tools like ACADO, CVXGEN, or OSQP generate highly optimised, library-free C code tailored to the MPC problem structure, enabling convex MPC on standard automotive ECUs.

  • Real-Time Iteration (RTI): As discussed in the NMPC chapter, the RTI scheme performs a single SQP step per sampling interval, trading optimality for guaranteed timing. This is the basis of the acados framework used in automotive and aerospace applications.

Where to Go from Here

These notes provide the foundations and several advanced extensions, but MPC is a vast and active research field. Directions not covered here include:

  • Stochastic MPC: When disturbances follow known probability distributions, chance constraints (\(\Pr(x_k \in \mathcal{X}) \ge 1 - \epsilon\)) can replace the worst-case guarantees of Tube MPC, often yielding less conservative behaviour. See Mesbah .

  • Distributed and Multi-Agent MPC: Large-scale systems (power grids, traffic networks, multi-robot teams) require decomposing the centralised MPC problem into smaller, coupled subproblems solved in parallel by local agents. See Christofides et al. .

  • Mixed-Integer MPC: Systems with discrete decisions (on/off actuators, gear selection, logical constraints) lead to mixed-integer programmes (MIPs) that are NP-hard but increasingly tractable with modern solvers like Gurobi.

  • Adaptive MPC: When the plant model changes over time (e.g., varying mass, ageing components), the controller must simultaneously identify the model and control the system. Adaptive MPC combines online system identification with robust or cautious MPC formulations.

Final Remarks

MPC has evolved from a niche technique in petrochemical process control to a universal framework for constrained optimal control. The combination of principled optimisation, explicit constraint handling, and the ability to incorporate models of arbitrary complexity makes it the method of choice for an ever-growing range of applications—from autonomous vehicles and rockets to buildings and biological systems. We hope these notes provide a solid foundation for both understanding the theory and applying it to real engineering problems.
