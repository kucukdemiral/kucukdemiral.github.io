Foreword

Model Predictive Control (MPC) is an optimisation-based control methodology that has become one of the most widely used advanced control methods. By explicitly exploiting a dynamical model of the system, MPC predicts future behaviour and determines control actions by solving a constrained optimisation problem in real time. This predictive and optimisation-driven nature distinguishes MPC fundamentally from classical feedback controllers such as PID or lead–lag compensators, which rely on fixed control laws designed offline.

Initially developed and deployed in the process industries, MPC has matured into a general-purpose control framework applicable across a wide range of engineering domains. Advances in numerical optimisation, computational power, and embedded hardware have enabled its adoption in robotics, autonomous vehicles, aerospace systems, power electronics, renewable energy systems, and smart infrastructure. In many of these applications, the ability of MPC to handle multivariable dynamics, operational constraints, and future information in a systematic manner is essential.

These lecture notes have been prepared for the Model Predictive Control module delivered as part of the MSc Applied Instrumentation and Control programme at Glasgow Caledonian University. The material has been designed with a strongly practical emphasis. The primary objective is to introduce MPC in a way that allows students to apply the techniques they learn directly to realistic engineering problems, rather than treating MPC as a purely theoretical topic. Wherever possible, abstract concepts are motivated using physical intuition, illustrative examples, and implementation-oriented discussions.

The notes are structured so that they can be followed either as guided lecture material or as a self-contained resource for independent study. A student who works through the notes sequentially should be able to acquire a working knowledge of MPC sufficient to model systems, formulate predictive control problems, and implement controllers using modern computational tools. To accommodate delivery within approximately three weeks, mathematical depth is deliberately balanced against clarity and applicability.

A note on the \(^{(*)}\) symbol. Chapters and sections marked with \(^{(*)}\) in their title contain supplementary material that is not covered during the timetabled lectures. Specifically, this includes the formal stability theory section in the MPC chapter (recursive feasibility, terminal sets, and the Lyapunov stability proof), the Simulink implementation chapter (Chapter [chap:simulink_mpc]), the Robust and Tube MPC chapter (Chapter [chap:tube_mpc]), and the course conclusion and further topics chapter (Chapter [chap:conclusion]). These chapters and sections are included for completeness and for students who wish to deepen their understanding independently. The core assessed material is contained in the unmarked chapters and sections.

Beyond the core assessed material, the notes include several supplementary chapters that extend the scope of the treatment. Robust and Tube MPC is developed in full, including set operations, constraint tightening, worked examples, and MATLAB implementations. The Simulink implementation chapter demonstrates a complete closed-loop MPC simulation for a building energy system. The concluding chapter introduces nonlinear MPC, learning-based MPC, and data-driven predictive control at a conceptual level, contextualising MPC within current research and industrial practice and providing a foundation for further study.

A basic background in SISO control systems, state-space representations, linear algebra, and numerical computation is assumed. Familiarity with MATLAB is strongly recommended, as examples and implementations throughout the notes rely on MATLAB-based workflows. Several examples also use the YALMIP optimisation toolbox, which is freely available at https://yalmip.github.io. With this background, the material should be accessible to postgraduate students in control, instrumentation, and related engineering disciplines. Two self-study appendices are included at the end of these notes: one reviewing the required linear algebra and one covering the probability theory used in the Kalman filter chapters. Distance learning students are encouraged to consult these appendices as needed.

The opening chapter introduces Model Predictive Control and contrasts it with classical control methodologies. The motivation for MPC is established by highlighting the limitations of traditional feedback controllers when faced with constraints, multivariable interactions, and the need for anticipation. The receding horizon principle is introduced and explained using intuitive analogies, and the core components of MPC—models, objectives, constraints, and feedback—are discussed. The chapter concludes with a detailed application example drawn from energy-efficient building control, demonstrating how MPC exploits disturbance forecasts and operational constraints in practice.

The second chapter focuses on optimisation, which forms the computational engine of MPC. The fundamental elements of optimisation problems are introduced, including decision variables, cost functions, and constraints. Key concepts such as convex sets, convex functions, and problem classifications are discussed with emphasis on intuition rather than formal proofs. Practical examples illustrate how optimisation problems arise naturally in engineering contexts and how they are solved using modern numerical tools.

The third chapter develops the state-space framework that underpins predictive control. Continuous-time and discrete-time models are reviewed, and methods for discretising linear and nonlinear systems are presented. The treatment includes exact discretisation of linear systems as well as numerical schemes for nonlinear dynamics. Basic notions of discrete-time stability are also introduced, providing essential background for predictive control design.

The fourth chapter revisits controllability and observability from a state-space perspective. These properties are examined in the context of predictive control and state estimation, and their practical significance is emphasised. Rank conditions are derived and interpreted, and computational verification using MATLAB is demonstrated.

The fifth chapter introduces linear quadratic regulation as a finite-horizon optimisation problem. By formulating LQR in this way, the close conceptual relationship between optimal control and MPC becomes more apparent. The solution via the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker conditions and the resulting Riccati recursion are discussed, along with infinite-horizon LQR and stabilisation. The chapter also addresses non-zero setpoint tracking, establishing ideas that are later reused in MPC formulations.

The sixth chapter is devoted to optimal state estimation and the Kalman filter. Stochastic system models and noise assumptions are introduced, followed by the derivation of the Kalman filter as an optimal estimator. The duality between estimation and control is highlighted, and practical issues related to tuning noise covariance matrices are discussed. This material provides the foundation for output-feedback MPC.

The seventh chapter presents the standard formulation of linear Model Predictive Control. The finite-horizon constrained optimisation problem is developed explicitly, and the receding horizon implementation is revisited from a computational standpoint. Efficient implementation strategies using optimisation modelling tools are demonstrated, and the influence of design parameters such as prediction horizon length and terminal cost weighting is discussed.

The eighth chapter extends MPC to output-feedback and offset-free formulations. Disturbance modelling and joint state–disturbance estimation are introduced, along with target calculation methods for reference tracking. Practical issues such as feasibility, soft constraints, and robustness to modelling errors are addressed, reflecting challenges commonly encountered in real-world applications.

The ninth chapter addresses the practical implementation of MPC within the Simulink environment. A general implementation framework is developed, covering the construction of YALMIP optimiser objects, the use of persistent variables for managing state across time steps, and strategies for overcoming code-generation constraints. The chapter explains how to assemble a complete closed-loop simulation without relying on the MATLAB MPC Toolbox, and demonstrates the methodology through a detailed case study of economic MPC applied to a four-zone building heating system. This case study integrates an augmented Kalman filter for joint state and disturbance estimation, an economic steady-state target calculator, and a deviation-form MPC regulator, and includes guidance on common implementation pitfalls and their remedies.

The tenth chapter develops robust MPC for systems subject to bounded disturbances. The limitations of standard MPC in the presence of model uncertainty are discussed, and the Tube MPC framework is presented in detail. The chapter introduces the Minkowski sum and Pontryagin difference as the essential set operations, derives the constraint tightening mechanism, and develops the complete Tube MPC optimisation problem. Worked examples for both scalar and multi-state systems are provided, along with MATLAB implementations using YALMIP and MPT3.

The concluding chapter provides a broader perspective on MPC and outlines directions beyond the core material. Topics such as nonlinear MPC, real-time and embedded implementation, and data-driven and learning-based predictive control are briefly introduced. The aim is to situate the material within the wider current state of control research and practice and to encourage further study of the subject.

Taken as a whole, these notes aim to connect theory and practice in Model Predictive Control. They are intended to equip students not only with an understanding of the underlying principles, but also with the confidence and practical skills required to apply MPC to complex, constrained engineering systems.
