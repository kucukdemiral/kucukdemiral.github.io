Optimisation: The Engine of MPC

“Since the fabric of the universe is most perfect and the work of a most wise Creator, nothing at all takes place in the universe in which some rule of maximum or minimum does not appear.”
— Leonhard Euler

In Chapter [chap:introduction], we introduced MPC as a controller that looks ahead and adjusts control actions by solving an optimisation problem at every time step. But this raises a fundamental question: how exactly does the computer decide which adjustment is the correct one?

A classical PID controller follows a simple, fixed rule: if the error increases, push back harder. It is reactive and rigid. An MPC controller, however, does not follow a pre-written rule. Instead, at every single time step, it faces a choice. It determines the best control action within the given constraints. This process of systematic selection is called optimisation.

Optimisation provides the mathematical framework for translating engineering objectives—such as drive fast but be safe—into precise numerical instructions that a computer can understand. If the system model is the map that tells the controller where it can go, optimisation is the navigator that decides where it should go.

In this chapter, we step away from system dynamics for a moment to focus purely on this decision-making engine. We will learn how to formulate engineering goals as objective functions, how to translate physical limits into mathematical constraints, and how to distinguish between problems that are easy for computers to solve and those that are computationally impossible.

What is Optimisation?

In a general sense, the Oxford Dictionary defines optimisation as:

  • “The action of making the best or most effective use of a situation or resource.”

In engineering, we translate this concept into a precise mathematical framework. We are not just “trying to do our best”; we are systematically searching for a specific numerical value that satisfies a mathematical criterion.

Mathematical Definition of Optimisation A numerical approach for seeking a best decision or action from a set of alternatives.

The Anatomy of an Optimisation Problem

Formally, optimisation equates to the minimisation (or maximisation) of an objective function \(J(x)\), depending on a variable vector \(x\), subject to constraints. The general mathematical problem is written as:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \min_{x} \quad & J(x) \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & x \in S \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

To translate a real-world engineering problem into the mathematical form described above, we must identify three fundamental components. Regardless of the complexity, almost every optimisation problem consists of:

  1. Decision Variables (\(x\)): These are the knobs the solver can turn to minimise the cost. In the context of MPC, these variables usually represent the sequence of future control inputs (e.g., valve positions, motor currents, steering angles) over the prediction horizon.

  2. Objective Function (\(J\)): A scalar mathematical expression that quantifies performance. The solver attempts to find the decision variables that result in the smallest possible value for this function. It typically represents physical quantities such as energy cost, tracking error, fuel consumption, or elapsed time.

  3. Constraints: The rules that must be obeyed. These define the feasible set (denoted as \(S\)). Constraints distinguish practical engineering from pure mathematics by strictly enforcing physical limits (e.g., maximum voltage, tank capacity) or safety bounds (e.g., obstacle avoidance).

Mathematically, we often denote the decision variables as \(z\) to distinguish them from the system state \(x\) used in control theory. The standard optimisation form is written as:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \min_{x} \quad & J (x) \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & g_i(x) \leq 0, \quad i=1,\ldots,m & \text{(Inequality constraints)} \\ & h_j(x) = 0, \quad j=1,\ldots,q & \text{(Equality constraints)} \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Study note: Constraint Types

  • Inequalities (\(g \le 0\)) describe ranges, like “speed must be less than 100 km/h”.

  • Equalities (\(h = 0\)) describe strict physics, like \(F = ma\). In MPC, the system dynamics (\(x_{k+1} = Ax_k + Bu_k\)) are treated as equality constraints. Note that here \(x\) is the state of the system and not a decision variable.

Illustrative Examples

To make the abstract definitions of decision variables, objective functions, and constraints concrete, let us consider two distinct optimisation problems.

The process of translating a physical or data-driven problem into a mathematical optimisation format is known as modelling. This step is often more challenging than the solution itself. In both examples below, we follow a systematic three-step approach:

  1. Identify the Decision Variables: What are the knobs we can turn?

  2. Formulate the Objective: What is the single metric that defines best?

  3. Define the Constraints: What are the physical or logical limits we must obey?

The first example (Box Volume) illustrates a geometric design problem where physical constraints play a critical role—analogous to actuator limits in control systems. The second example (Linear Regression) introduces the concept of least-squares minimisation, which is the mathematical foundation for data science.

Maximising Box Volume Problem Statement: Given a square piece of cardboard with dimensions \(1\,\text{m} \times 1\,\text{m}\) as shown in Figure [fig:box_volume], we wish to construct an open-top box with the maximum possible volume. We achieve this by cutting equal squares of side length \(x\) from the four corners and folding up the sides.

We map this problem to the three components of optimisation:

  1. Decision Variable (\(x\)): The size of the corner cut.

  2. Constraints:

    • The cut must be positive: \(x \geq 0\).

    • The cut cannot overlap (total width is 1m): \(2x \leq 1 \implies x \leq 0.5\).

  3. Objective Function: Maximise Volume \(V = \text{length} \times \text{width} \times \text{height}\). \[J(x) = \underbrace{(1-2x) \cdot (1-2x)}_{\textbf{Base Area}} \cdot x = x(1-2x)^2\]

Mathematical Formulation:1 \[\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \max_{x} \quad & x(1-2x)^2 \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & 0 \leq x \leq 0.5 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}\] or equivalently \[\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \min \quad & -x(1-2x)^2 \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & 0 \leq x \leq 0.5 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}\]

Linear Regression (Least Squares) Problem Statement: Suppose we are given a dataset containing \(N\) pairs of experimental measurements: \(\{(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), \dots, (x_N, y_N)\}\) as shown in Figure [fig:linear_regression].

We want to find the linear model (a straight line) that "best fits" this data.

Note that in this specific problem, the \(x\) values are constants (data we measured), not decision variables.

  1. Decision Variables: The parameters of the line: the slope vector \(a \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and the intercept \(b \in \mathbb{R}\).

    • Note: In the 2D plot above, \(n=1\), so \(a\) is a scalar slope. However, the math holds for \(n\)-dimensional vectors.

  2. Objective Function: We define the prediction for the \(i\)-th data point as \(\hat{y}_i = a^T x_i + b\). We want to minimise the Sum of Squared Errors (SSE): \[J(a, b) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - (a^T x_i + b) \right)^2\]

  3. Constraints: In this case, \(a\) and \(b\) can be any real numbers. There are no restrictions. Therefore this is an unconstrained optimisation.

Mathematical Formulation:
\[\begin{equation*} \min_{a, b} \quad \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - (a^T x_i + b) \right)^2 \end{equation*}\]

Ingredients of Multi-Variable Optimisation

In the previous examples (the box and the line fitting), we dealt with specific cases. Now, let us formalise the general structure of an optimisation problem involving multiple variables. This is the exact structure used in Model Predictive Control, where we must simultaneously optimise a sequence of inputs over time.

Problem Setting: The Decision Vector

In real-world engineering, we rarely make a single scalar decision. Instead, we must determine a set of \(n\) values simultaneously. To handle this mathematically, we collect all individual decision variables (\(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\)) into a single decision vector: \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\): \[\begin{equation} x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}\] Then the objective function \(J\) maps this vector to a scalar real number (a score or cost): \[J: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\] For example, in MPC, if we have a control horizon of 10 steps and 2 inputs, our decision vector \(x\) has a dimension of \(n=20\).

Understanding Vector-to-Scalar Mapping: The cost function

The mathematical notation \(J: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\) often confuses beginners. It reads: "The function \(f\) takes a vector of \(n\) numbers and transforms it into a single real number."

To understand why this is necessary, let us consider a relatable real-world analogy: The Shopping Basket.

The Analogy

Imagine you are at a supermarket checkout.

  1. The Vector (\(x\)): Your shopping basket contains many different items. You might have 2 loaves of bread, 6 eggs, and 1 litre of milk. We can list these quantities as a vector: \[x = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 6 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{matrix} \leftarrow \text{Bread} \\ \leftarrow \text{Eggs} \\ \leftarrow \text{Milk} \end{matrix}\] This vector represents the state of your shopping. It is multi-dimensional because it contains different types of information.

  2. The Objective Function (\(J\)): The checkout till scans your items. It applies a specific rule (the price list) to your vector. It multiplies each item by its value and sums them up: \[J(x) = (2 \times \text{£}1.00) + (6 \times \text{£}0.20) + (1 \times \text{£}0.80)\]

  3. The Scalar (\(J\)): The till produces a single number: £4.00.

This is visually described in Figure [fig:cost_function_analogy].

Why is this important in Engineering?

You cannot easily say which of two shopping baskets is larger just by looking at the items—one might have more eggs but no milk. However, once you map them to a scalar (the total price), comparison becomes easy. You can instantly say which basket costs less.

In Model Predictive Control, our basket (\(x\)) contains complex decisions: valve positions, heater settings, and motor speeds over the next 20 minutes. We cannot simply look at two different plans and say Plan A is better than Plan B, because Plan A might use less fuel but Plan B might track the temperature better.

The objective function \(J(x)\) squashes all these complex, multi-dimensional decisions into a single number (a score).

  • If Plan A scores \(10.5\) and Plan B scores \(8.2\), and we are minimising cost, we know for a fact that Plan B is the mathematical winner.

Key Takeaway Optimisation algorithms cannot minimise a list or a vector. They can only minimise a single number. The objective function acts as a translator, converting a complex list of engineering decisions into a single score that allows us to rank them.

Constraints and the Feasible Set

We rarely operate in an ideal world. Our decisions are restricted by physical limits. These restrictions are defined by a finite number of equations or inequalities involving the decision variables.

  1. Inequality Constraints (\(g_i\)): These represent bounds, such as "Temperature must be less than \(100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\)" or "Valve must be open at least 10%". \[g_i(x) \leq b_i, \quad i = 1, \dots, m\]

  2. Equality Constraints (\(h_j\)): These represent strict physics or model dynamics, such as Input flow must equal output flow or \(F = ma\). \[h_j(x) = 0, \quad j = 1, \dots, q\]

  3. Domain Restrictions: Sometimes variables are not continuous. For instance, a switch is either ON (1) or OFF (0). If \(p\) variables are discrete (integers) and the rest are continuous, we denote the domain as \(x \in \mathbb{R}^{n-p} \times \mathbb{Z}^p\). Note that dealing with domain restrictions is difficult in optimisation.

Collectively, these constraints define a specific region in the \(n\)-dimensional space called the Feasible Set \(S\). The optimisation algorithm is only allowed to search for solutions within this set. \[\begin{equation} S = \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid g_i(x) \leq b_i \ \forall i, \ h_j(x) = 0 \ \forall j \} \end{equation}\] If the intersection of these constraints is empty (e.g., \(x > 5\) AND \(x < 3\)), the problem is infeasible—there is no solution.

Visualising the Feasible Set Consider a 2-dimensional problem (\(x \in \mathbb{R}^2\)) with two inequality constraints:

  1. \(g_1(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2 \leq 4\) (Must be inside a circle of radius 2).

  2. \(g_2(x) = x_1^3 - x_2 \leq 0 \implies x_2 \geq x_1^3\) (Must be above the cubic curve).

The feasible set \(S\) is the intersection of these two regions (the purple area as shown in Figure [fig:intersection_of_constraints]).

Standard Form: Minimisation vs. Maximisation

Standard numerical solvers (like quadprog or fmincon in MATLAB) are typically written to perform minimisation. However, engineering problems are often phrased as maximisation (e.g., maximise profit, maximise volume).

To reconcile this, we rely on a mathematical identity. But first, we must clarify a precise piece of notation often used in optimisation: the difference between \(\min\) and \(\arg \min\).

Notation Note: Minimum vs. Argument of the Minimum:

  • \(\min f(x)\): Returns the value of the function at the minimum. This is the score (the vertical axis on a graph).

  • \(\arg \min f(x)\): Returns the argument (the variable \(x\)) that produces that minimum. This is the decision (the horizontal axis).

In MPC, we are primarily interested in the \(\arg \min\) because we need to know what input \(u\) to apply to the system, not just what the theoretical cost is.

Converting Maximisation to Minimisation:
With this notation in mind, we can state the equivalence principle. We do not need separate software to solve maximisation problems. We rely on the identity that finding the argument that maximises a function is equivalent to finding the argument that minimises its negative:

\[\begin{equation} \underbrace{\arg \max_{x \in S} f(x)}_{\text{Best Decision}} \equiv \underbrace{\arg \min_{x \in S} \{-f(x)\}}_{\text{Equivalent Search}} \end{equation}\]

Visually, multiplying the objective function by \(-1\) flips the curve upside down. A peak (maximum) in the original function becomes a valley (minimum) in the negated function. While the vertical values flip signs, the horizontal location (\(x\)) of the optimal point remains exactly the same. This is pictured in Figure 1.1.

Therefore, in this course, we will consistently formulate our MPC problems as minimisation tasks, usually minimising an energy or error cost function.

Terminology in Optimisation

With the general structure of an optimisation problem in place, we now refine the vocabulary. Some optimisation problems can be solved in milliseconds on a cheap microcontroller, whilst others might take days on a supercomputer and still fail to find the best solution.

The distinguishing factor is rarely just the size of the problem (the number of variables); rather, it is the geometry of the problem.

The difference between easy and hard problems lies not in the number of variables but in the geometry of the search space. The central concept here is Convexity: if the objective is convex and the constraints form a convex set, then any local minimum is guaranteed to be the global minimum. We begin by defining the building blocks of this geometry: sets and functions.

Convex Sets

A fundamental concept in optimisation, particularly in Model Predictive Control (MPC), is that of a convex set. Working with convex sets and functions simplifies the optimisation problem significantly, often guaranteeing that any local optimum is also a global optimum.

Convex Set A set \(\mathcal{X}\) is convex if and only if for any pair of points \(x\) and \(y\) in \(\mathcal{X}\), any convex combination of \(x\) and \(y\) lies in \(\mathcal{X}\).

Mathematically, this definition is stated as: \[\mathcal{X} \text{ is convex} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \lambda x + (1 - \lambda)y \in \mathcal{X}, \quad \forall \lambda \in [0, 1], \quad \forall x, y \in \mathcal{X}\] The term \(\lambda x + (1 - \lambda)y\) represents a line segment connecting \(x\) and \(y\).

Interpretation All line segments starting and ending in \(\mathcal{X}\) stay within \(\mathcal{X}\).

To illustrate the concept, consider the following examples of convex and non-convex sets.

In the context of Model Predictive Control (MPC), constraints are often defined by linear inequalities. Geometrically, these constraints represent hyperplanes and half-spaces. Understanding these geometric primitives is essential because they form the building blocks of convex sets.

Hyperplane A hyperplane is a set of the form: \[\begin{equation} \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid a^\top x = b\} \end{equation}\] where \(a \in \mathbb{R}^n\) (with \(a \neq 0\)) and \(b \in \mathbb{R}\). The vector \(a\) is the normal vector to the hyperplane; it is orthogonal to any vector lying within the hyperplane.

In \(\mathbb{R}^2\), a hyperplane defines a line. In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), it defines a plane. This is shown on the left of Figure 1.3.

Half space A hyperplane divides the space \(\mathbb{R}^n\) into two halves. A half-space is the set of points on one side of (or on) the hyperplane: \[\begin{equation} \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid a^\top x \leq b\} \end{equation}\] This is known as a closed half-space because it includes the boundary (the hyperplane itself). If the inequality is strict (\(<\)), it is an open half-space. This is shown on the right of Figure 1.3.

Hyperplanes and half-spaces are always convex sets. This property ensures that optimisation problems with linear constraints remain convex.

Linear Constraint in \(\mathbb{R}^2\)

Consider a linear relation defined by the equation: \[\begin{equation} 2x_1 + 1x_2 = 2 \end{equation}\] This represents a line in 2D space. To relate this to the standard hyperplane definition \(a^\top x = b\), we define the vectors: \[\begin{equation} a = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad x = \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad b = 2 \end{equation}\] Thus, the equation becomes: \[\begin{equation} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}}_{a^\top} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix} = \underbrace{2}_{b} \end{equation}\]

Connection to the Gradient:
Readers needing a refresher on vectors, matrices, and eigenvalues may consult Appendix [app:linalg].

We can also view this through the lens of multivariate calculus. Let us define a function \(f(x)\) implicitly by the zero-level set: \[\begin{equation} f(x) = 2x_1 + x_2 - 2 = 0 \end{equation}\] The gradient of this function, denoted as \(\nabla f\), is a column vector containing the partial derivatives with respect to \(x_1\) and \(x_2\): \[\begin{equation} a = \nabla_x f = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\[12pt] \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}\] This confirms that the vector \(a = (2, 1)^\top\) is the gradient of the linear function defining the hyperplane. Geometrically, the gradient vector is perpendicular to the level sets of the function, meaning \(a\) is perpendicular to the line \(2x_1 + x_2 = 2\), as shown in Figure [fig:example_hyperplane].

Polyhedron Let \(a_i \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and \(b_i \in \mathbb{R}\) for \(i=1,\dots,m\). A polyhedron \(P \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) is the intersection of a finite number of closed half–spaces, \[P := \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid a_i^\top x \le b_i,\; i = 1,\dots,m \}.\] If we collect the row vectors \(a_i^\top\) into a matrix \(A := [a_1,\dots,a_m]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) and \(b := [b_1,\dots,b_m]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^m\), then we may write \[P := \{ x \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid Ax \le b\},\] where the inequality is understood row–wise.

Polytope A polytope is a bounded polyhedron, that is, a polyhedron which is contained in a ball of finite radius.

Important observation Any intersection of convex sets is convex. Each closed half–space \(\{x \mid a_i^\top x \le b_i\}\) is convex, therefore every polyhedron (and hence every polytope) is a convex set. However, union of convex sets are not convex in general.

Numerical Example: Defining a Polytope

Consider the set \(P \subset \mathbb{R}^2\) defined by the inequalities \[\begin{align*} x_1 &\ge 0, & x_2 &\ge 0, \\ x_1 &\le 3, & x_2 &\le 2, \\ x_1 + x_2 &\le 4. \end{align*}\] We can rewrite these in the standard form \(Ax \le b\) as \[A= \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad b= \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 3 \\ 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad P := \{x\in\mathbb{R}^2 \mid Ax \le b\}.\] The intersection of these half–spaces is a bounded polygon with vertices \[\{(0,0),\ (3,0),\ (3,1),\ (2,2),\ (0,2)\},\] so \(P\) is a polytope.

Convex Functions

Let \(S\) be a convex subset of a vector space. We define the properties of functions mapping \(S\) to the real numbers \(\mathbb{R}\) as follows.

Convex Function A function \(f: S \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is said to be convex if \(S\) is convex and the following inequality holds for all \(z_1, z_2 \in S\) and for all \(\lambda \in [0, 1]\): \[\begin{equation} f(\lambda z_1 + (1 - \lambda)z_2) \leq \lambda f(z_1) + (1 - \lambda)f(z_2) \end{equation}\] Geometrically, this means that the line segment connecting any two points on the graph of the function lies above or on the graph between the two points.

Concave Function A function \(f: S \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is concave if \(S\) is convex and \(-f\) is a convex function. Equivalently, the inequality in the definition of convexity is reversed.

The concept of convexity is illustrated in Figure 1.4. The curve represents the function \(f\). The red secant line connects two points \((z_1, f(z_1))\) and \((z_2, f(z_2))\). For a convex function, the value of the function evaluated at a weighted average of two points (the black dot on the curve) is always less than or equal to the weighted average of the function values (the black dot on the red line).

Classification of Optimisation Problems

In the previous sections, we defined the general anatomy of an optimisation problem (Variables, Objectives, Constraints). However, in engineering practice, identifying the specific class of a problem is critical.

Different classes of problems require different numerical algorithms (solvers). For instance, solving a linear problem is instantaneous, whereas solving a non-convex nonlinear problem might take hours or fail completely. If we can mathematically massage our engineering problem to fit a standard form, we can solve it efficiently using off-the-shelf software.

We typically classify problems in two ways:

  1. By the existence and type of constraints (Constrained vs. Unconstrained).

  2. By the mathematical structure of the cost and constraint functions (Linear, Quadratic, Nonlinear).

Classification I: Existence of Constraints

The most fundamental distinction is whether the decision variables are restricted by boundaries.

Unconstrained Optimisation

An unconstrained problem is one where the decision variables can take any value in the Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^n\). There are no boundaries, fences, or rules limiting \(x\).

\[\begin{equation} \min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} J(x) \end{equation}\]

Visualising Unconstrained Problems: Level Curves

When dealing with \(n\) variables, the function \(J(x)\) describes a surface in \(n+1\) dimensions. This is hard to visualise. Instead, we use Level Curves (or contour lines). A level curve is a hypersurface defined by \(J(x) = c\), where \(c\) is a constant scalar. This is exactly like looking at a topographical map of a mountain; each line represents a constant height.

Unconstrained Example: The Bowl Consider the simple quadratic function: \[J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\] Here, \(x = [x_1, x_2]^\top\). We want to find the minimum. The level curves are circles where \(x_1^2 + x_2^2 = c\).

  • If \(c=5\), the curve is a circle of radius \(\sqrt{5}\).

  • If \(c=0\), the curve is a single point at the origin.

Figure [fig:level_curves] shows several level curves \(J=2\), \(J=6\), \(J=12\) and \(J=20\).

The optimiser searches for the level curve with the smallest possible \(c\). Geometrically, this is the centre of the concentric circles.

Constrained Optimisation

In most engineering applications, particularly MPC, we are restricted. Valves cannot open more than 100%, and tanks cannot hold negative volume. This leads to constrained optimisation, where \(x\) must belong to a specific feasible set \(S\). We classify these based on how the set \(S\) is defined.

Type A: Equality Constraints

An equality constrained problem restricts the decision variables to lie strictly on a specific curve or surface (\(h(x)=0\)). The solver is not allowed to choose any point off this line. Mathematically, this reduces the dimension of the search space.

Equality Constraints: The Tangent Point Problem Statement: Minimise the same quadratic function as before, but subject to a linear equality constraint.

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} & \min_{x} \quad J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2 \\ \text{subject to:} &\\ & h(x) = x_1 + 2x_2 - 4 = 0 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}\]

Visualisation: This problem is visualised in Figure [fig:optimization_with_constraints].

The optimal point is found where the constraint line is exactly tangent to a level curve.

Type B: Inequality Constraints

Unlike equality constraints, inequality constraints confine the solution to a region (a set of alternatives). The optimal solution may lie on the boundary of this region (active constraint) or strictly inside it (inactive constraint).

Inequality Constraints: The Feasible Region Minimise the cost function \(J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\), subject to four linear inequalities: \[\begin{aligned} -x_1 - 2x_2 + 5 &\leq 0 \\ 2x_1 + x_2 - 5 &\leq 0 \\ x_1 \ge 0, \ x_2 &\ge 0 \end{aligned}\]

Visualisation is excellent for understanding, but to get numerical answers, we use software. In this course, we utilise YALMIP, an open source MATLAB toolbox that allows us to write optimisation problems using syntax that looks almost identical to the mathematics.

The workflow always follows three steps:

Define Optimisation Variables \(\to\) Define Model \(\to\) Solve.

        % 'sdpvar' creates a symbolic variable that YALMIP tracks.
        x = sdpvar(2,1); 
        
        % 2. Define the Objective Function
        J = x(1)^2 + x(2)^2;
        
        % 3. Define Constraints
        % We create a list of inequalities using standard MATLAB brackets.
        Constraints = [ -x(1) - 2*x(2) + 5 <= 0, ...
                                2*x(1) +   x(2) - 5 <= 0, ...
                                x(1) >= 0, ...
                                x(2) >= 0 ];
        
        % 4. Call the Solver
        % We ask YALMIP to minimise J subject to Constraints
        optimize(Constraints, J);
        
        % 5. Extract Result
        % The symbolic variable 'x' now holds the optimal value.
        x_star = value(x) % Result: (1, 2)

Visualisation: Geometrically, the optimiser “expands” the level curves (blue circles) from the origin until they first touch the feasible region (grey polygon).

Type C: General Constraints

In the most general case, a problem contains both equality and inequality constraints. The solver must find a solution that lies inside the inequality region and lies exactly on the equality curve.

Geometrically, the feasible set is the intersection of the regions defined by all constraints. This often effectively reduces the dimensionality of the search space.

General Constraints: Mixing Equalities and Inequalities Problem Statement: Consider a scenario where we must satisfy both safety limits (inequalities) and strict physical laws (equalities). We wish to minimise the quadratic cost \(J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\) subject to: \[\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \text{s.t.} \quad & -x_1 - 2x_2 + 5 \leq 0 \\ & 2x_1 + x_2 - 5 \leq 0 \\ & x_1 \ge 0, \quad x_2 \ge 0 \\ & x_1 + 2x_2 - 7 = 0 \quad {(Equality)} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}\]

YALMIP Implementation: When translating this into code, pay close attention to the syntax for the equality constraint. In MATLAB, a single equals sign (=) represents assignment (e.g., setting a variable’s value). To represent a logical equality condition (\(h(x)=0\)), we must use the double equals sign (==).

        x = sdpvar(2,1);
        J = x(1)^2 + x(2)^2;
        
        % 2. Define Inequality Constraints (The Feasible Region)
        g1 = [-x(1) - 2*x(2) + 5 <= 0]; 
        g2 = [ 2*x(1) + x(2) - 5 <= 0]; 
        g3 = [x(1) >= 0];               
        g4 = [x(2) >= 0];     
        
        % 3. Define Equality Constraint (The Manifold)
        % Note the use of '==' for logical equality
        h  = [x(1) + 2*x(2) - 7 == 0];
        
        % 4. Combine and Solve
        Constraints = [g1, g2, g3, g4, h];
        optimize(Constraints, J);
        
        x_star = value(x) % Result: (1, 3)

Visualisation and Analysis: The geometry of this problem is illustrated in Figure [fig:Intersection_of_constraints].

  • The inequalities form the grey triangular region.

  • The equality constraint is represented by the orange dashed line.

A valid solution must satisfy both conditions. Therefore, the feasible set is reduced to the specific line segment where the orange line slices through the grey triangle.

The Cost of Constraints: In the previous example (inequalities only), the optimiser could reach the point \((1,2)\), which yielded a cost of \(J=5\).

However, the new equality constraint forces the solution to lie on the line \(x_1 + 2x_2 = 7\). The point \((1,2)\) is no longer valid because \(1 + 2(2) = 5 \neq 7\). The solver is forced to move further away from the origin to satisfy this new rule, settling at \((1,3)\) with a higher cost of \(J=10\).

This illustrates a fundamental rule of engineering optimisation: Adding constraints generally worsens (increases) the minimum cost, as it restricts the freedom of the solver to find "cheaper" solutions.

Classification II: Mathematical Structure

Optimisation problems vary greatly in difficulty depending on the structure of their objective functions and constraints. In this section we review some of the most commonly encountered problem classes, beginning with those that are considered easy from a computational point of view, and progressing to problems that are, in general, far more challenging to solve.

Linear Programmes (LPs)

Linear Programmes constitute one of the most fundamental classes of convex optimisation problems. Both the objective and the constraints are linear: \[\begin{align*} &\min_{x} \; c^{\top} x \\ \text{subject to: } & \\ &Gx \le h \\ &Ax = b \end{align*}\]

The feasible set of an LP is a polyhedron, and the optimum, when it exists, is attained at an extreme point (vertex) of this polyhedron. Linear optimisation on a polytope may be visualised as shifting parallel hyperplanes of constant cost until they touch the feasible region. Figure 1.5 shows a 2D example where the polytope corresponds to the closed area modelled by \(Gx\le h\) and dashed lines shows the cost for different values of \(c^\top x\).

Linear Programme: Minimum-Effort Steady State

The Goal: Consider a discrete-time linear system \[x_{k+1} = Ax_k + Bu_k\] We wish to find a steady-state pair \((x_{\mathrm{ss}}, u_{\mathrm{ss}})\) (an equilibrium point where the system stays at rest (\(x_{k+1} = x_k\))) subject to safety constraints.

Crucially, among all possible valid equilibria, we want to find the one that requires the least control effort. In engineering (e.g., satellite thrusters), "effort" is often quantified by fuel consumption, which corresponds to the sum of the absolute values of the inputs.

1. The 1-Norm and Notation: Mathematically, we want to minimise the 1-norm of the input vector \(u \in \mathbb{R}^m\): \[\begin{equation} \|u\|_1 = \sum_{i=1}^{m} |u_i| = |u_1|+|u_2|+\cdots + |u_m| \end{equation}\] However, the absolute value function \(|u|\) is non-linear. Standard LP solvers cannot handle it directly. To solve this, we use a standard optimisation trick involving the vector \(1\).

  • Let \(1\) be a column vector of ones: \(1 = [1, 1, \dots, 1]^\top\).

  • Then \(1^\top t\) is simply the sum of the elements of vector \(t\): \(\sum t_i\).

2. The Epigraph Trick: We introduce an auxiliary decision vector \(t \in \mathbb{R}^m\). We constrain \(t\) to be an upper bound on the magnitude of \(u\): \[\begin{equation} -t_i \le u_i \le t_i \implies |u_i| \le t_i \end{equation}\] If we minimise the sum of elements in \(t\) (i.e., \(\min 1^\top t\)), the solver will squeeze \(t_i\) down until \(t_i = |u_i|\). This effectively minimises \(|u_i|\) using only linear inequalities.

3. The Formulation: We can now pose the full Linear Programme. The decision variables are the input \(u\), the state \(x_{\mathrm{ss}}\), and the auxiliary bound \(t\).

\[\begin{aligned} &\min_{u,\, x_{\mathrm{ss}},\, t} \quad 1^\top t && \text{(Minimise total fuel/effort)}\\ \text{subject to:} &&& \\ & (I-A)x_{\mathrm{ss}} - Bu = 0, && \text{(Equilibrium Physics)}\\ & G\,x_{\mathrm{ss}} \le h, && \text{(Safety Constraints)}\\ & u_{\min} \le u \le u_{\max}, && \text{(Actuator Limits)}\\ & -t \le u \le t. && \text{(Norm Linearisation)} \end{aligned}\]

This formulation allows us to find the most efficient operating point for the system using extremely fast and reliable Linear Programming solvers.

Stigler’s Diet Problem:

The Stigler Diet Problem is a classic linear optimisation problem. The objective is to determine the most cost-effective daily diet that meets specific nutritional requirements using a limited set of foods.

In this simplified 2D version, we consider two food items: Corn and Wheat Bread. We aim to minimise the total daily cost while satisfying constraints for Energy (Calories) and Vitamin A.

We first define the problem using general parameters and units.

Decision Variables:

  • \(x_1\): Quantity of Corn [kg]

  • \(x_2\): Quantity of Bread [kg]

Parameters:

  • \(c_1, c_2\): Price of food [$/kg]

  • \(k_1, k_2\): Energy content [kcal/kg]

  • \(v_1, v_2\): Vitamin A content [\(\mu\)g/kg]

  • \(K_{min}, K_{max}\): Daily energy limits [kcal]

  • \(V_{min}, V_{max}\): Daily Vitamin A limits [\(\mu\)g]

Optimisation Model: \[\begin{align*} \underset{x_1,\,x_2}{\text{min}} \quad & J(x) = c_1 x_1 + c_2 x_2 \\ \text {Subject to:} \quad & K_{min} \leq k_1 x_1 + k_2 x_2 \leq K_{max} \\ & V_{min} \leq v_1 x_1 + v_2 x_2 \leq V_{max} \\ & x_1 \geq 0, \quad x_2 \geq 0 \end{align*}\]

Using the specific data provided for the problem:

Data:

  • Costs: \(c_1 = 2.0\), \(c_2 = 0.5\)

  • Energy: \(k_1 = 750\), \(k_2 = 650\) (Target: \(2000 - 3000\) kcal)

  • Vitamin A: \(v_1 = 250\), \(v_2 = 0\) (Target: \(500 - 800\) \(\mu\)g)

Numerically, the problem can be stated as follows:

\[\begin{align*} &\underset{x_1,x_2}{\min } \quad J(x) = 2x_1 + 0.5x_2 & \\ \text{Subject to:} &&\\ &2000 \leq 750x_1 + 650x_2 \leq 3000 & \text{(Energy)} \\ &500 \leq 250x_1 \phantom{{} + 650x_2} \leq 800 & \text{(Vitamin A)} \\ & x_1 \geq 0, \quad x_2 \geq 0 & \text{(Nonnegativity)} \end{align*}\] Figure 1.6 shows the feasible solution set along with the optimal solution that minimises the cost of nutrition.

    x2 = sdpvar(1); % Bread (kg)

    else
        disp('Problem could not be solved.');
    end

Convex Quadratic Programmes (QPs)

Quadratic Programmes (QPs) extend linear optimisation by allowing a quadratic objective function whilst retaining linear equality and inequality constraints. A QP takes the general form \[\begin{align*} &\min_{x} \quad \frac{1}{2} x^{\top} P x + q^{\top} x \\ \text{subject to: } & \\ & Gx \le h \\ & Ax = b \end{align*}\]

where, \(x\in \mathbb R^n\), \(P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\), \(q \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \(G \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\), and \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times n}\). The feasible region defined by the linear constraints is always a polyhedron.

The QP is convex if and only if the Hessian matrix \(P\) is positive semidefinite: \[P \succeq 0 .\] This is shown in Figure 1.7.

Tests for Positive Semidefiniteness (\(P \succeq 0\)) For a symmetric matrix \(P\), the following statements are equivalent and may be used to verify that \(P\) is positive semidefinite:

  1. Eigenvalues: All eigenvalues of \(P\) are non-negative: \[\lambda_i(P) \ge 0, \qquad i=1,\dots,n.\]

  2. Quadratic Form: The quadratic form is non-negative for every vector \(z \in \mathbb{R}^n\): \[z^\top P z \ge 0, \qquad \forall z \in \mathbb{R}^n.\]

  3. Principal Minors: All principal minors of \(P\) are non-negative. In other words, the determinant of every principal submatrix must satisfy \[\det(P_{\mathcal I,\mathcal I}) \ge 0\] for every index set \(\mathcal I \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}\).

Important note: For positive definite matrices \((P \succ 0)\), Sylvester’s criterion states that it is enough to check that all leading principal minors are strictly positive. However, for positive semidefinite matrices \((P \succeq 0)\), checking only the leading principal minors is not sufficient in general; one must check all principal minors, or use one of the equivalent tests above.

(For a review of eigenvalues, definiteness, and related matrix concepts, see Appendix [app:linalg].)

Hessian The Hessian matrix, denoted as \(\nabla^2 f(x)\) or \(H(x)\), collects all second-order partial derivatives of a scalar-valued function \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\). If \(x = [x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n]^\top\), the element in the \(i\)-th row and \(j\)-th column is given by: \[\begin{equation*} H_{ij} = \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j} \end{equation*}\]

Example: Hessian Consider a simple function of two variables: \[f(x) = 3x_1^2 + 2x_1 x_2 + 5x_2^2\] First, we find the gradient (first derivatives): \[\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} = 6x_1 + 2x_2, \quad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} = 2x_1 + 10x_2\] Differentiating again gives the Hessian matrix: \[H = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1 \partial x_2} \\[12pt] \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2 \partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_2^2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 & 2 \\ 2 & 10 \end{bmatrix}\] Note that for smooth functions, the Hessian is always a symmetric matrix (\(H_{ij} = H_{ji}\)).

In the strictly convex case (where \(P \succ 0\)), the level sets of the objective function form concentric ellipses. This structure guarantees that the optimisation problem yields a unique global minimiser, provided the feasible region is non-empty.

Geometrically, the solver searches for the smallest possible ellipse that still overlaps with the constraints. The optimal solution is found at the precise point where this lowest-cost contour touches (or grazes) the feasible polytope. This graphical interpretation is illustrated in Figure 1.7.

Convex QPs benefit from strong theoretical guarantees and can be solved efficiently using interior-point, active-set, or first-order optimisation methods. They are widely used in control, signal processing, finance, estimation, and engineering design.

Example: Robot Distance to a Restricted Zone Scenario: Let a robot is located at coordinates \(p = (3, 2)\). A restricted geometric zone \(\mathcal{X}\) (representing an obstacle or a target landing pad) is defined as a unit box centered at the origin. The robot must compute the shortest distance to this zone to plan its trajectory.

Optimisation Problem: Find the point \(x \in \mathcal{X}\) closest to \(p\): \[\begin{equation*} \min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^2} \; (x_1 - 3)^2 + (x_2 - 2)^2 \end{equation*}\]

Constraints (Standard Format): The unit box is defined by bounds \(-1 \leq x_1 \leq 1\) and \(-1 \leq x_2 \leq 1\). In standard linear inequality form \(Ax \leq b\), these are written as: \[\begin{align*} x_1 &\leq 1 \\ -x_1 &\leq 1 \\ x_2 &\leq 1 \\ -x_2 &\leq 1 \end{align*}\]

Visualising the Solution: By inspecting the geometry (see Figure [fig:qp_distance]), the closest point within the box to \((3,2)\) is the upper-right corner of the box, \(x^* = (1, 1)\).

To solve this problem numerically using a solver (like OSQP, quadprog, or GUROBI), we must map the problem into the standard Quadratic Programming form: \[\begin{equation} \min_{x} \; \frac{1}{2} x^\top P x + q^\top x \quad \text{subject to} \quad Gx \leq h \end{equation}\]

We expand the squared Euclidean distance objective: \[\begin{align*} J(x) &= (x_1 - 3)^2 + (x_2 - 2)^2 \\ &= (x_1^2 - 6x_1 + 9) + (x_2^2 - 4x_2 + 4) \\ &= \underbrace{x_1^2 + x_2^2}_{\text{Quadratic part}} \underbrace{- 6x_1 - 4x_2}_{\text{Linear part}} + \underbrace{13}_{\text{Constant}} \end{align*}\] The constant term (\(13\)) does not affect the optimal location \(x^*\) and is discarded. To match the standard form \(\frac{1}{2} x^\top P x\), the matrix \(P\) must be scaled such that the diagonal elements are 2. \[\frac{1}{2} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix}}_{x^\top} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}}_{P} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}}_{x} + \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} -6 & -4 \end{bmatrix}}_{q^\top} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}\] Thus, we define: \[P = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad q = \begin{bmatrix} -6 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}\]

The unit box constraints are: \[-1 \leq x_1 \leq 1 \quad \text{and} \quad -1 \leq x_2 \leq 1\] These must be rewritten as "less than or equal to" inequalities (\(Gx \leq h\)): \[\begin{align*} x_1 &\leq 1 \\ -x_1 &\leq 1 \\ x_2 &\leq 1 \\ -x_2 &\leq 1 \end{align*}\] In matrix notation, this becomes: \[\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}}_{G} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} \leq \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}}_{h}\]

MATLAB Implementation using YALMIP

Having derived the standard QP matrices manually, we can now solve the problem using MATLAB. The following code demonstrates YALMIP’s symbolic framework to automate the process.

clear; clc;
    ops = sdpsettings('solver', 'quadprog', 'verbose', 0);
    
    p = [3; 2];          % Robot position
    x = sdpvar(2, 1);    % Variable
    
    Objective  = (x(1) - p(1))^2 + (x(2) - p(2))^2;
    
    Constraints = [-1 <= x <= 1];
    
    optimize(Constraints , Objective, ops);
    
    x_opt = value(x);
    fprintf('Optimal Sol : x1=%.2f, x2=%.2f\n', x_opt(1), x_opt(2));

Direct Implementation using quadprog

While YALMIP offers a convenient high-level interface, it is essential to understand how to pass the problem matrices directly to a solver. This effectively opens the "black box" of the optimisation tool.

In MATLAB, the native solver quadprog2 solves problems of the form: \[\begin{equation*} \min_x \frac{1}{2}x^\top H x + f^\top x \quad \text{subject to} \quad A x \leq b \end{equation*}\] This syntax requires a direct mapping from our mathematical notation (\(P, q, G, h\)) to MATLAB’s standard arguments (\(H, f, A, b\)).

clear; clc;
    
    % 1. Map the Mathematical Notation to MATLAB Syntax
    % Objective: 1/2 * x'*P*x + q'*x
    H = [2, 0; 
            0, 2];          % Our Matrix P
    f = [-6; -4];       % Our Vector q
    
    % Constraints: G*x <= h
    A = [ 1,  0; 
            -1,  0; 
            0,  1; 
            0, -1];       % Our Matrix G
    b = [1; 1; 1; 1];   % Our Vector h
    
    % 2. Set Options (optional, to suppress command window output)
    options = optimoptions('quadprog', 'Display', 'off');
    
    % 3. Call the Solver
    % Syntax: x = quadprog(H, f, A, b)
    x_opt = quadprog(H, f, A, b, [], [], [], [], [], options);
    
    % 4. Display Results
    fprintf('Optimal Sol : x1=%.2f, x2=%.2f\n', x_opt(1), x_opt(2));

Nonconvex Quadratic Programmes

Not all optimisation problems retain convexity. Once convexity is lost, global optimality becomes substantially harder to guarantee. While standard MPC relies on convex optimisation, certain control formulations lead to nonconvex problems. A specific class of these "hard" problems is the Nonconvex Quadratic Programme (QP).

When the matrix \(P\) in the quadratic objective is not positive semidefinite (\(P \not\succeq 0\)), the objective function is nonconvex. This often manifests as saddle-shaped level sets. Consequently, the feasible polytope may contain multiple local minima, making the global optimum difficult to find efficiently.

The formulation is given by: \[\begin{align*} &\min_x \quad \frac{1}{2} x^{\top} Px + q^{\top} x\\ \text{subject to: }& \\ &Gx \le h \\ & Ax = b \end{align*}\]

Mixed-Integer Linear Programmes (MILPs)

A Mixed-Integer Linear Programme retains the linear objective and constraints of an LP but imposes integrality restrictions on some or all decision variables: \[\begin{align*} &\min_{x} \quad c^{\top} x \\ \text{subject to: } & \\ &Gx \le h \\ &Ax = b \\ &x \in \{0,1\}^n \;\; \text{or} \;\; x \in \mathbb{Z}^n . \end{align*}\]

The feasible set becomes a set of discrete points embedded within a polyhedron, leading to a combinatorial explosion in complexity. Figure 1.9 shows a sketch of a possible mixed-integer problem.

MILP: Industrial Energy Management

The General Problem: Consider an industrial site supplied by the electrical grid and equipped with a dispatchable generator and a battery energy storage system. The objective is to minimise the total operating cost over a finite horizon of \(N\) discrete time steps of duration \(\Delta t\), while meeting the site demand and respecting operational constraints.

The generator can be switched on or off and incurs both fuel and start-up costs. The battery is subject to energy dynamics and cannot charge and discharge simultaneously.

For each time step \(k=1,\dots,N\), define: \[\begin{align*} p_g(k) &\ge 0 && \text{generator power (kW)},\\ p_{\mathrm{grid}}(k) &\ge 0 && \text{grid import power (kW)},\\ p_{\mathrm{ch}}(k),\, p_{\mathrm{dis}}(k) &\ge 0 && \text{battery charge/discharge power (kW)},\\ E(k) & && \text{battery energy (kWh)},\\ u(k) &\in \{0,1\} && \text{generator on/off status},\\ s(k) &\in \{0,1\} && \text{generator start-up indicator},\\ z(k) &\in \{0,1\} && \text{battery charge mode indicator}. \end{align*}\]

Objective function: The total operating cost is given by \[\begin{equation} \min \sum_{k=1}^{N} \Delta t \left( c_{\mathrm{grid}}(k)\,p_{\mathrm{grid}}(k) + c_f\,p_g(k) \right) + \sum_{k=1}^{N} c_{\mathrm{start}}\, s(k), \end{equation}\] where \(c_{\mathrm{grid}}(k)\) denotes the time-varying electricity price, \(c_f\) the generator fuel cost coefficient, and \(c_{\mathrm{start}}\) the generator start-up cost.

Constraints:

Power balance: \[\begin{equation} p_{\mathrm{grid}}(k) + p_g(k) + p_{\mathrm{dis}}(k) - p_{\mathrm{ch}}(k) = d(k), \quad \forall k. \end{equation}\] The term \(d(k)\) represents the exogenous electrical demand (load) of the system at time step \(k\), assumed to be known over the prediction horizon.

Generator operating limits: \[\begin{equation} P_{g,\min} u(k) \le p_g(k) \le P_{g,\max} u(k), \quad \forall k. \end{equation}\]

Generator start-up logic: \[\begin{align} s(1) &\ge u(1),\\ s(k) &\ge u(k) - u(k-1), \quad k=2,\dots,N. \end{align}\] where \(s(k)\) is a binary variable that equals one if the generator is started at time \(k\), i.e. when the on/off status \(u(k)\) transitions from 0 to 1.

Battery energy dynamics: \[\begin{equation} E(k+1) = E(k) + \Delta t\,\eta_{\mathrm{ch}}\,p_{\mathrm{ch}}(k) - \Delta t\,\frac{1}{\eta_{\mathrm{dis}}}\,p_{\mathrm{dis}}(k), \end{equation}\] with \(E(1)=E_0\). Also, \(\eta_{\mathrm{ch}}\in(0,1]\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{dis}}\in(0,1]\) denote the charging and discharging efficiencies of the battery, respectively.

Battery and grid limits: \[\begin{align} E_{\min} &\le E(k) \le E_{\max},\\ 0 &\le p_{\mathrm{ch}}(k) \le P_{\mathrm{ch},\max},\\ 0 &\le p_{\mathrm{dis}}(k) \le P_{\mathrm{dis},\max},\\ 0 &\le p_{\mathrm{grid}}(k) \le P_{\mathrm{grid},\max}. \end{align}\]

Nonsimultaneous charging and discharging: \[\begin{align} p_{\mathrm{ch}}(k) &\le P_{\mathrm{ch},\max} z(k),\\ p_{\mathrm{dis}}(k) &\le P_{\mathrm{dis},\max} [1-z(k)]. \end{align}\]

Solving this problem: The model above is a Mixed-Integer Linear Programme (MILP) due to the binary variables \(u(k), s(k), z(k)\). It requires specialised solvers like Gurobi or CPLEX. In the software section later, we will implement a simplified version of this (a continuous LP) to demonstrate the concept in MATLAB.

Software Tools for Optimisation

Implementing Model Predictive Control (MPC) requires a robust software stack. Generally, the implementation workflow is divided into two distinct layers:

  1. Parsers (Modelling Languages): High-level interfaces that allow the user to define the optimisation problem using intuitive algebraic syntax (e.g., x^2 + y^2 <= 1). The parser translates this symbolic representation into the standard numerical matrix formats required by the solver.

  2. Solvers: The low-level numerical engines that perform the mathematical iterations to find the optimal solution (e.g., Active-Set methods or Interior-Point methods).

Modelling Languages (Parsers)

Choosing the right parser depends heavily on the programming environment (MATLAB vs. Python) and the type of problem (Convex vs. Nonlinear).

For MATLAB users, YALMIP is the de facto standard for prototyping. It supports a vast array of solvers and allows users to switch between them by changing a single line of code. CVX is another popular option, specifically designed for Disciplined Convex Programming (DCP), a methodology that requires users to compose their optimisation problems from a fixed library of convex functions and composition rules so that convexity is automatically verified.

For Python users, CVXPY is the equivalent of CVX, providing a clean object-oriented interface for convex problems.

For Nonlinear MPC (NMPC) and high-performance applications, CasADi is widely used in both Python and MATLAB. Unlike standard parsers, CasADi implements Algorithmic Differentiation (AD), allowing it to compute exact gradients and Hessians automatically, which is critical for solving nonlinear control problems efficiently.

Solving Optimisation Problems: From Theory to Code

Mathematical formulation is only half the battle. To deploy Model Predictive Control, we must translate these equations into code that a computer can solve. In this section, we explore a complex energy management system as an example.

Economic MPC for Smart Grids

In this example, we explore a sophisticated application of optimisation: Economic Energy Management. The goal here is purely economic: minimise the total cost of electricity over a 24-hour horizon.

Problem Scenario

Although the general problem formulation (the MILP discussed earlier) included complex generator logic, for this MATLAB example we will solve a simplified but highly relevant subset: Battery Energy Arbitrage.

Consider a home with:

  • Load (\(P_{load}\)): The household power consumption (lighting, appliances).

  • Solar Generation (\(P_{solar}\)): Free renewable energy available during the day.

  • Battery Storage: A unit capable of storing energy (\(E_{bat}\)) to bridge the gap between supply and demand.

  • Grid Connection: Allows buying power when needed or selling excess power.

The Challenge: Electricity prices fluctuate throughout the day (Time-of-Use pricing). Prices are low at night and high in the evening. We must decide the optimal battery charge/discharge schedule to minimise the daily bill.

Mathematical Formulation

We define the discrete time step \(\Delta t = 1\) hour and the horizon \(N=24\) hours.

1. Variables:

  • \(P_{grid}(k)\): Power exchanged with the grid (kW). Positive = Buy, Negative = Sell.

  • \(P_{bat}(k)\): Battery charging power (kW). Positive = Charge, Negative = Discharge.

  • \(E_{bat}(k)\): Energy stored in the battery (kWh).

2. The Objective Function: Minimise the cumulative cost of grid usage: \[\begin{equation} \min_{P_{bat}, P_{grid}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \text{Price}(k) \times P_{grid}(k) \cdot \Delta t \end{equation}\]

3. Constraints: \[\begin{align} \text{Power Balance:} \quad & P_{grid}(k) + P_{solar}(k) = P_{load}(k) + P_{bat}(k) \\ \text{Dynamics:} \quad & E_{bat}(k+1) = E_{bat}(k) + P_{bat}(k) \cdot \Delta t \\ \text{Capacity Limits:} \quad & E_{min} \leq E_{bat}(k) \leq E_{max} \\ \text{Power Limits:} \quad & P_{min} \leq P_{bat}(k) \leq P_{max} \end{align}\]

Implementation (MATLAB & YALMIP)

We use YALMIP to translate this word problem into a numerical format. Since this problem is purely continuous, we solve it as a Linear Programme (LP) using quadprog (with zero quadratic cost) or linprog.

    N = 24;                 % Horizon (24 hours)
    E_cap = 13.5;           % Battery Capacity (kWh, e.g., Tesla Powerwall)
    P_max = 5;              % Max Charge/Discharge rate (kW)
    dt = 1;                 % Time step (1 hour)

    % Price: 0.10 (Off-peak), 0.30 (Peak: 17:00-21:00)

    t = (0:N-1)';
    Solar = max(0, 4 * sin(pi * (t - 6) / 12));

    Load = 1 + 2 * exp(-(t-19).^2/10) + 1 * exp(-(t-8).^2/5);

    P_grid = sdpvar(N, 1);   % Grid Power
    P_bat  = sdpvar(N, 1);   % Battery Power

    C = [C, E_bat(1) == 0];

Numerical Methods for Optimisation\(^{(*)}\)

The previous sections defined the components of an optimisation problem—decision variables, cost function, and constraints—and classified common problem types (LP, QP, MILP). This section addresses a natural follow-up question: how do numerical solvers actually find the optimal solution? We begin with the simplest case, finding roots of scalar functions, and progressively build toward multi-variable constrained optimisation algorithms. The methods introduced here underpin virtually every solver used in Model Predictive Control.

The key insight linking root finding to optimisation is the first-order necessary condition: for \(x^*\) to be a local minimum of a differentiable function \(J(x)\), the gradient must vanish, \[\nabla J(x^*) = 0.\] Therefore, finding an optimum reduces to finding a root of the gradient. We begin by studying two classical root-finding algorithms—bisection and Newton–Raphson—before generalising them to multi-variable optimisation.

The Bisection Method

Bisection Method Given a continuous function \(g\colon \mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) and an interval \([a,b]\) such that \(g(a)\cdot g(b)<0\), the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees at least one root \(x^*\in(a,b)\). The bisection method evaluates \(g\) at the midpoint \(c=(a+b)/2\) and replaces the endpoint whose function value has the same sign as \(g(c)\), thereby halving the bracket at each step.

Convergence rate: linear. After \(k\) iterations the error satisfies \[|x_k - x^*| \;\le\; \frac{b-a}{2^{k}}.\]

Algorithm 1: Bisection Method

Require: \(g\), \(a\), \(b\), tolerance \(\varepsilon > 0\) with \(g(a)\cdot g(b) < 0\)

1: while \((b - a) > \varepsilon\) do

2:  \(c \gets (a+b)/2\)

3:  if \(g(a)\cdot g(c) < 0\) then

4:   \(b \gets c\)

5:  else

6:   \(a \gets c\)

7:  end if

8: end while

9: return \((a+b)/2\)

Root Finding by Bisection Consider finding the roots of the polynomial \[g(x) \;=\; 3x^2 - 6x + 2 \;=\; 0.\] This arises, for instance, when setting the gradient of a cost function to zero. We observe that \(g(0)=2>0\) and \(g(0.5)=-0.25<0\), so by the IVT a root lies in \([0,\,0.5]\).

Newton–Raphson Method

Newton–Raphson Iteration We seek \(x^*\) such that \(g(x^*)=0\), but we cannot solve this equation directly. Instead, we approximate \(g\) near the current iterate \(x_k\) using a first-order Taylor expansion: \[g(x) \;\approx\; g(x_k) + g'(x_k)\,(x - x_k).\] Setting this linear approximation to zero and solving for \(x\) gives the next iterate: \[0 = g(x_k) + g'(x_k)\,(x_{k+1} - x_k) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad {\;x_{k+1} \;=\; x_k \;-\; \frac{g(x_k)}{g'(x_k)}\;}\] Geometrically, this replaces the curve \(g\) by its tangent line at \(x_k\) and takes the root of that tangent as the next iterate. The process repeats, producing progressively better approximations.

Convergence is quadratic near the root: there exists a constant \(C>0\) such that \[|x_{k+1}-x^*| \;\le\; C\,|x_k-x^*|^2.\] This means the number of correct digits roughly doubles at each step.

Newton–Raphson for Root Finding We apply Newton–Raphson to the same function \(g(x)=3x^2-6x+2\), with derivative \(g'(x)=6x-6\). Starting from \(x_0=0\):

0 \(0.0000\) \(+2.0000\) \(-6.0000\) \(0.3333\)
1 \(0.3333\) \(+0.3333\) \(-4.0000\) \(0.4167\)

Newton–Raphson converges to six-decimal accuracy in only 3 iterations, compared with roughly 20 iterations for bisection. This dramatic speedup is a direct consequence of the quadratic convergence rate: once the iterates are close to the root, the number of correct digits approximately doubles at each step.

Connection to Optimisation When we apply the Newton–Raphson method to the first-order optimality condition \(J'(x)=0\), the update formula becomes \[x_{k+1} \;=\; x_k \;-\; \frac{J'(x_k)}{J''(x_k)}.\] This is Newton’s method for one-dimensional optimisation. In the next subsection, we generalise this idea to multiple dimensions: the scalar derivative \(J'\) is replaced by the gradient \(\nabla J\), and the scalar second derivative \(J''\) is replaced by the Hessian matrix \(\nabla^2 J\).

Unconstrained Optimisation: Gradient-Based Methods

We now generalise the root-finding ideas from one dimension to multiple dimensions. Given a differentiable cost function \(J\colon\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}\), the necessary condition for a local minimum is \(\nabla J(x^*)=0\). The gradient \(\nabla J(x)\in\mathbb{R}^n\) points in the direction of steepest ascent; its negative \(-\nabla J(x)\) is the direction of steepest descent. This observation motivates the iterative algorithms below.

Gradient Descent (Steepest Descent)

Gradient Descent Starting from an initial guess \(x_0\in\mathbb{R}^n\), the gradient descent method generates iterates \[x_{k+1} \;=\; x_k \;-\; \alpha\,\nabla J(x_k),\] where \(\alpha>0\) is the step size (or learning rate). If \(\alpha\) is sufficiently small and \(J\) is smooth, the cost decreases monotonically: \(J(x_{k+1})<J(x_k)\).

Algorithm 2: Gradient Descent

Require: \(J\), \(\nabla J\), initial guess \(x_0\), step size \(\alpha > 0\), tolerance \(\varepsilon > 0\)

1: \(k \gets 0\)

2: while \(\|\nabla J(x_k)\| > \varepsilon\) do

3:  \(x_{k+1} \gets x_k - \alpha\,\nabla J(x_k)\)

4:  \(k \gets k + 1\)

5: end while

6: return \(x_k\)

A single step size \(\alpha\) cannot simultaneously suit both scales.

Step-Size Selection: Armijo Backtracking

A fixed step size \(\alpha\) is fragile—too large causes divergence, too small makes convergence impractically slow. The Armijo backtracking line search adaptively selects \(\alpha\) at each step to guarantee sufficient decrease.

Armijo Condition A step size \(\alpha\) satisfies the Armijo (sufficient decrease) condition if \[J(x_k + \alpha\, d_k) \;\le\; J(x_k) \;+\; c\,\alpha\,\nabla J(x_k)^\top d_k,\] where \(d_k\) is the search direction (e.g. \(d_k=-\nabla J(x_k)\)) and \(c\in(0,1)\) is a small constant (typically \(c=10^{-4}\)). The right-hand side is a linear model of the decrease; the condition requires the actual decrease to be at least as large as a fraction \(c\) of the predicted decrease.

Algorithm 3: Armijo Backtracking Line Search

Require: \(J\), \(\nabla J\), current iterate \(x_k\), search direction \(d_k\)

Require: \(c = 10^{-4}\), contraction factor \(\beta = 0.5\)

1: \(\alpha \gets 1\)

2: while \(J(x_k + \alpha\, d_k) > J(x_k) + c\,\alpha\,\nabla J(x_k)^\top d_k\) do

3:  \(\alpha \gets \beta\,\alpha\)

4: end while

5: return \(\alpha\)

Relevance to MPC In real-time Nonlinear MPC (Chapter 10), Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) solvers such as SNOPT and IPOPT use globalised Newton steps with Armijo-type line searches. This prevents the solver from overshooting, which could violate safety constraints on a physical system. The backtracking mechanism is therefore not merely a numerical convenience—it is a practical safeguard.

Newton’s Method for Optimisation

Gradient descent uses only first-order information (the gradient). Newton’s method also uses second-order information (the Hessian), enabling much faster convergence by accounting for the curvature of \(J\).

Newton’s Method for Optimisation Starting from \(x_0\in\mathbb{R}^n\), Newton’s method generates iterates by solving the linear system \[\nabla^2 J(x_k)\, d_k \;=\; -\nabla J(x_k)\] for the Newton direction \(d_k\), then updating \(x_{k+1}=x_k+\alpha_k\, d_k\) (with \(\alpha_k\) chosen by an Armijo line search, or \(\alpha_k=1\) for a full Newton step). Near a local minimum with a positive-definite Hessian, convergence is quadratic.

Algorithm 4: Newton's Method for Optimisation

Require: \(J\), \(\nabla J\), \(\nabla^2 J\), initial guess \(x_0\), tolerance \(\varepsilon > 0\)

1: \(k \gets 0\)

2: while \(\|\nabla J(x_k)\| > \varepsilon\) do

3:  Solve \(\nabla^2 J(x_k)\, d_k = -\nabla J(x_k)\) for \(d_k\)

4:  \(\alpha_k \gets \text{ArmijoBacktrack}(J, \nabla J, x_k, d_k)\)

5:  \(x_{k+1} \gets x_k + \alpha_k\, d_k\)

6:  \(k \gets k + 1\)

7: end while

8: return \(x_k\)

Newton vs. Gradient Descent We apply Newton’s method to the same problem: \(J(x)=5x_1^2+x_2^2\), starting from \(x_0=(4,\,3)^\top\).

The gradient and Hessian are \[\nabla J(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 10x_1 \\ 2x_2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \nabla^2 J = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(constant)}.\] At \(x_0=(4,3)^\top\): \[\nabla J(x_0) = \begin{bmatrix} 40 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad d_0 = -\bigl(\nabla^2 J\bigr)^{-1}\nabla J(x_0) = -\begin{bmatrix} 1/10 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 40 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}.\] With a full Newton step (\(\alpha=1\)): \[x_1 = x_0 + d_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = x^*.\] Newton’s method converges in one step because \(J\) is quadratic and the Hessian is constant. Gradient descent required 20+ iterations for comparable accuracy.

This observation is directly relevant to MPC: the standard MPC cost function is a convex quadratic programme (QP), so Newton-type solvers (e.g. active-set or interior-point methods) converge in very few iterations per active-set change.

x = [4; 3]; tol = 1e-8;
H = [10 0; 0 2];              % Hessian (constant for this quadratic)
J = @(z) 5*z(1)^2 + z(2)^2;  % cost function
for k = 1:50
    g = [10*x(1); 2*x(2)];    % gradient
    if norm(g) < tol, break; end
    d = -H \ g;                % Newton direction
    alpha = 1;                 % Armijo backtracking
    while J(x + alpha*d) > J(x) + 1e-4*alpha*(g'*d)
        alpha = 0.5 * alpha;
    end
    x = x + alpha * d;
    fprintf('k=%d: x=[%.4f, %.4f], J=%.6f\n', k, x(1), x(2), J(x));
end

Constrained Optimisation: Lagrange Multipliers

The unconstrained methods we have studied so far find minima by solving \(\nabla J(x^*) = 0\). However, in Model Predictive Control, we always operate under constraints—dynamics equations impose equality constraints, while actuator limits and safety bounds impose inequalities. How do we find a minimum when the optimum is forced to lie on a constraint surface?

Geometric Intuition: Tangency of Level Curves

Consider minimising \(J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\) subject to \(x_1 + 2x_2 = 4\). The level curves of \(J\) are concentric circles centred at the origin. The constraint is a straight line. As we move along the constraint line, \(J\) decreases until we reach a point where the constraint is tangent to a level curve—this is the constrained minimum. At this special point, the gradient of \(J\) is parallel to the gradient of the constraint: \[\nabla J(x^*) = -\lambda \,\nabla h(x^*) \quad \text{for some scalar } \lambda.\] The key geometric insight is that if \(\nabla J\) had any component along the constraint surface, we could slide along the constraint and further reduce \(J\). Only when the gradient of \(J\) is entirely perpendicular to the constraint (i.e., parallel to \(\nabla h\)) is there no improving direction remaining.

The Lagrangian Function

The geometric observation—that \(\nabla J\) and \(\nabla h\) must be parallel at the optimum—can be formalised algebraically by introducing an auxiliary variable \(\lambda\) called the Lagrange multiplier.

The Lagrangian and First-Order Conditions For the equality-constrained problem \[\min_{x}\; J(x) \quad \text{subject to} \quad h(x) = 0,\] define the Lagrangian: \[\mathcal{L}(x,\lambda) \;=\; J(x) + \lambda^\top h(x).\] At a constrained minimum \((x^*,\lambda^*)\), the following first-order necessary conditions hold: \[\begin{align*} \nabla_x \mathcal{L} &= \nabla J(x^*) + \lambda^{*\top} \nabla h(x^*) = 0 && \text{(Stationarity)}\\ h(x^*) &= 0 && \text{(Feasibility)} \end{align*}\] This is a system of \((n+q)\) equations in \((n+q)\) unknowns: \(n\) primal variables \(x\) and \(q\) multipliers \(\lambda\).

The Lagrange multiplier \(\lambda\) has an important economic interpretation: it measures the sensitivity of the optimal cost to a perturbation of the constraint. If we relax the constraint to \(h(x) = \epsilon\), the optimal cost changes by approximately \(\lambda\,\epsilon\). In MPC, this tells us how much the cost would improve if we slightly relaxed an actuator limit or a state bound—a quantity known as the shadow price.

Lagrange Multipliers: Worked Example Minimise \(J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\) subject to \(h(x) = x_1 + 2x_2 - 4 = 0\).

Step 1: Form the Lagrangian. \[\mathcal{L}(x,\lambda) = x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \lambda(x_1 + 2x_2 - 4).\]

Step 2: First-order conditions. \[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_1} &= 2x_1 + \lambda = 0 &&\Longrightarrow \quad x_1 = -\tfrac{\lambda}{2}, \\[4pt] \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_2} &= 2x_2 + 2\lambda = 0 &&\Longrightarrow \quad x_2 = -\lambda, \\[4pt] \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \lambda} &= x_1 + 2x_2 - 4 = 0 &&\text{(feasibility)}. \end{align*}\]

Step 3: Substitute into the feasibility condition. \[\bigl(-\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\bigr) + 2(-\lambda) = 4 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad -\tfrac{5\lambda}{2} = 4 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \lambda^* = -\tfrac{8}{5} = -1.6.\]

Step 4: Back-substitute. \[x_1^* = -\frac{-1.6}{2} = 0.8, \qquad x_2^* = -(-1.6) = 1.6.\]

The constrained minimum is \(x^* = (0.8,\; 1.6)\) with optimal cost \(J^* = 0.64 + 2.56 = 3.20\).

Interpretation: \(\lambda^* = -1.6\) means that perturbing the constraint to \(x_1 + 2x_2 = 4 + \epsilon\) would increase the optimal cost by approximately \(1.6\epsilon\). This is intuitive: the unconstrained minimum is the origin, so pushing the constraint line further away forces a larger cost.

The Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) Conditions

The Lagrange multiplier method handles equality constraints beautifully, but most practical problems—especially in MPC—involve inequality constraints: actuator saturations (\(|u| \le u_{\max}\)), state bounds (\(x_{\min} \le x \le x_{\max}\)), and safety regions. The Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions extend Lagrange multipliers to handle both equality and inequality constraints simultaneously.

Active and Inactive Constraints

Consider an inequality constraint \(g_i(x) \le 0\). At a candidate solution \(x^*\), two situations arise:

  • Active (binding): \(g_i(x^*) = 0\)—the constraint is tight, and \(x^*\) lies on the constraint boundary. This constraint actively shapes the solution.

  • Inactive: \(g_i(x^*) < 0\)—the constraint is satisfied with slack. Removing it would not change the solution.

The fundamental insight is that active constraints behave like equality constraints at the optimum (because \(g_i = 0\)), while inactive constraints can be ignored. The KKT conditions formalise this observation by introducing a multiplier for each inequality and a rule that automatically “switches off” inactive constraints.

Statement of the KKT Conditions

Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) Necessary Conditions Consider the general constrained problem: \[\min_{x}\; J(x) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad g_i(x) \le 0,\; i=1,\ldots,m, \quad h_j(x) = 0,\; j=1,\ldots,q.\] If \(x^*\) is a local minimum satisfying a constraint qualification, then there exist multipliers \(\mu_i \ge 0\) and \(\lambda_j\) such that:

  1. Stationarity: \[\nabla J(x^*) + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \mu_i \,\nabla g_i(x^*) + \sum_{j=1}^{q} \lambda_j \,\nabla h_j(x^*) = 0\]

  2. Primal feasibility: \[g_i(x^*) \le 0, \qquad h_j(x^*) = 0\]

  3. Dual feasibility: \[\mu_i \ge 0\]

  4. Complementary slackness: \[\mu_i \, g_i(x^*) = 0 \quad \forall\, i\]

Complementary Slackness Demystified Condition 4 encodes the active/inactive logic. For each inequality constraint, exactly one of two cases holds:

  • \(g_i(x^*) = 0\) — the constraint is active and its multiplier \(\mu_i\) may be positive, or

  • \(\mu_i = 0\) — the constraint is inactive and exerts no influence.

A useful analogy: imagine a ball rolling downhill inside a fenced yard. If the ball comes to rest against a fence (active constraint, \(g_i = 0\)), the fence pushes back with a reaction force \(\mu_i > 0\)—without it, the ball would roll further. If the ball settles in the middle of the yard, far from any fence (inactive, \(g_i < 0\)), that fence exerts no force at all (\(\mu_i = 0\))—the ball does not even “know” it is there. The product \(\mu_i\,g_i = 0\) captures precisely this: either you are at the fence and it pushes, or you are away from it and it does nothing.

When Are KKT Conditions Sufficient? In general, the KKT conditions are only necessary—they identify candidate solutions but do not guarantee a global minimum. However, for convex problems (convex \(J\), convex feasible set), the KKT conditions are both necessary and sufficient. Since the standard MPC problem is a convex QP, any point satisfying the KKT conditions is the global optimum. This is precisely what solvers like quadprog exploit.

Geometric Interpretation

The stationarity condition has a powerful geometric meaning: at the constrained optimum, the negative gradient of the cost (the “desired descent direction”) must be expressible as a non-negative combination of the active constraint gradients. In other words, every direction that would decrease \(J\) violates at least one active constraint—you are “stuck” at the optimum.

When the optimum lies at the intersection of two or more active constraints, the descent direction must lie inside the cone spanned by their gradient vectors. The following figure illustrates this vertex case.

KKT Conditions: Complete Worked Example Minimise \(J(x) = (x_1 - 4)^2 + (x_2 - 3)^2\) subject to \(x_1 + x_2 \le 5\), \(x_1 \ge 0\), \(x_2 \ge 0\).

Step 1: Rewrite constraints in standard form \(g_i(x) \le 0\). \[\begin{align*} g_1(x) &= x_1 + x_2 - 5 \le 0, \\ g_2(x) &= -x_1 \le 0, \\ g_3(x) &= -x_2 \le 0. \end{align*}\]

Step 2: Form the Lagrangian. \[\mathcal{L} = (x_1-4)^2 + (x_2-3)^2 + \mu_1(x_1+x_2-5) + \mu_2(-x_1) + \mu_3(-x_2).\]

Step 3: Stationarity conditions. \[\begin{align} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_1} &= 2(x_1 - 4) + \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0 \tag{i} \\[4pt] \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_2} &= 2(x_2 - 3) + \mu_1 - \mu_3 = 0 \tag{ii} \end{align}\]

Step 4: Apply complementary slackness. We hypothesise that only \(g_1\) is active (\(\mu_2 = \mu_3 = 0\)): \[\begin{align*} \text{From (i):} &\quad 2(x_1-4) + \mu_1 = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x_1 = 4 - \tfrac{\mu_1}{2}, \\[3pt] \text{From (ii):} &\quad 2(x_2-3) + \mu_1 = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x_2 = 3 - \tfrac{\mu_1}{2}, \\[3pt] \text{From } g_1 \text{ active:} &\quad x_1 + x_2 = 5 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \bigl(4 - \tfrac{\mu_1}{2}\bigr) + \bigl(3 - \tfrac{\mu_1}{2}\bigr) = 5 \\ &\quad\Longrightarrow\quad 7 - \mu_1 = 5 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mu_1 = 2. \end{align*}\]

So \(x_1^* = 3\), \(x_2^* = 2\), \(\mu_1^* = 2\).

Step 5: Verify all KKT conditions.

  • Stationarity: \(2(3-4)+2-0 = 0\) and \(2(2-3)+2-0 = 0\)

  • Primal feasibility: \(3+2-5 = 0 \le 0\), \(-3 \le 0\), \(-2 \le 0\)

  • Dual feasibility: \(\mu_1 = 2 \ge 0\), \(\mu_2 = 0\), \(\mu_3 = 0\)

  • Complementary slackness: \(\mu_1 g_1 = 2 \cdot 0 = 0\), \(\mu_2 g_2 = 0\), \(\mu_3 g_3 = 0\)

Optimal cost: \(J^* = (3-4)^2 + (2-3)^2 = 1 + 1 = 2\).

Interpretation: \(\mu_1 = 2\) means that relaxing the budget constraint by \(\epsilon\) (allowing \(x_1 + x_2 \le 5 + \epsilon\)) would reduce the cost by approximately \(2\epsilon\).

How Solvers Work: Interior-Point and Active-Set Methods

The KKT conditions tell us what the optimal solution looks like. But how do practical QP solvers—the workhorses behind every MPC controller—actually find that solution? Two dominant algorithmic paradigms exist: the active-set method and the interior-point method. Understanding their mechanics helps MPC engineers choose the right solver, diagnose convergence failures, and exploit warm-starting.

The Active-Set Method

The active-set method is motivated by a simple observation: if we knew which inequality constraints were active (binding) at the optimum, we could replace them with equalities and solve an equality-constrained QP—a problem that reduces to a single linear system. Of course, we do not know the active set in advance, so the algorithm discovers it iteratively.

The method maintains a working set \(\mathcal{W}_k\): the subset of inequality constraints currently treated as equalities. At each iteration, the algorithm performs the following steps:

  1. Solve the equality-constrained subproblem. With the constraints in \(\mathcal{W}_k\) enforced as equalities (and all other inequality constraints temporarily ignored), solve the resulting KKT system to obtain a candidate step \(d_k\) and the associated Lagrange multipliers \(\mu_i\) for \(i \in \mathcal{W}_k\).

  2. If \(d_k = 0\) (no improving direction exists within the working set):

    • Check the multipliers. If \(\mu_i \geq 0\) for all \(i \in \mathcal{W}_k\), the KKT conditions are satisfied—stop, we are optimal.

    • If some \(\mu_j < 0\), then constraint \(j\) was incorrectly assumed active. Remove it from \(\mathcal{W}_k\) and repeat.

  3. If \(d_k \neq 0\) (an improving direction exists):

    • Take a step along \(d_k\), using the largest step length \(\alpha_k \in (0, 1]\) that does not violate any inactive constraint.

    • If an inactive constraint becomes active (a blocking constraint), add it to \(\mathcal{W}_k\).

    • Update the iterate: \(x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k d_k\).

Geometrically, the method walks along edges and vertices of the feasible polytope, much like the simplex method for linear programmes. Each iteration either moves to a new vertex (adding or dropping a constraint from the working set) or confirms optimality. For convex QPs, the algorithm terminates in a finite number of iterations—bounded by \(\binom{m}{n}\) working-set combinations in the worst case, but typically far fewer in practice.

Warm-Starting in MPC Between consecutive MPC time steps, the optimal solution shifts only slightly (the system has moved by one time step). The active set from the previous solution is almost always correct for the new problem. Solvers like qpOASES exploit this by warm-starting: initialising the working set from the previous solution, so that only 1–3 iterations are needed to solve the new QP. This is what makes active-set methods the preferred choice for real-time linear MPC, achieving solve times well below 1 ms for moderate problem sizes.

The Interior-Point (Barrier) Method

The interior-point method takes a contrasting philosophy to the active-set approach: instead of walking along the boundary of the feasible region, it approaches the optimum from the interior. The key idea is to replace the hard inequality constraints \(g_i(x) \leq 0\) with a smooth penalty that becomes infinite at the boundary, keeping all iterates strictly inside the feasible region.

Concretely, the inequality-constrained problem is approximated by the barrier problem: \[\begin{equation} \min_{x} \; J(x) - t \sum_{i=1}^{m} \ln\bigl(-g_i(x)\bigr), \label{eq:barrier_problem} \end{equation}\] where \(t > 0\) is the barrier parameter. The logarithmic term \(-t \ln(-g_i(x))\) is finite only when \(g_i(x) < 0\) (strictly feasible) and blows up to \(+\infty\) as \(g_i(x) \to 0^-\), creating an invisible repulsive wall at each constraint boundary.

For each fixed \(t > 0\), the barrier problem [eq:barrier_problem] is an unconstrained (or equality-constrained) smooth problem that can be solved by Newton’s method. The solution \(x^*(t)\) lies in the strict interior of the feasible set. As \(t \to 0\), the barrier penalty weakens and \(x^*(t)\) approaches the true constrained optimum \(x^*\). The curve traced by \(x^*(t)\) as \(t\) decreases is called the central path.

A crucial advantage of interior-point methods is their worst-case complexity: \(O(\sqrt{m} \cdot \log(1/\varepsilon))\) Newton steps suffice to reach \(\varepsilon\)-optimality, where \(m\) is the number of inequality constraints. This polynomial-time guarantee contrasts with the potentially exponential worst case of active-set methods (though active-set methods are typically faster in practice for small, warm-started problems).

Barrier Method: A 2D Illustration Minimise \(J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\) subject to \(x_1 + x_2 \geq 1\), \(x_1 \geq 0\), \(x_2 \geq 0\).

Rewriting in standard form \(g_i(x) \leq 0\): \[\begin{align*} g_1(x) &= 1 - x_1 - x_2 \leq 0, \\ g_2(x) &= -x_1 \leq 0, \\ g_3(x) &= -x_2 \leq 0. \end{align*}\]

The barrier problem becomes: \[\min_{x} \; x_1^2 + x_2^2 - t\bigl[\ln(x_1 + x_2 - 1) + \ln(x_1) + \ln(x_2)\bigr].\]

By symmetry, \(x_1 = x_2\) at every barrier optimum (and at the true constrained optimum). The true constrained optimum is \(x^* = (0.5,\; 0.5)\) with \(J^* = 0.5\).

Setting the gradient of the barrier objective to zero and exploiting symmetry, we can solve for \(x^*(t)\) analytically. The results for decreasing values of \(t\) are:

\(t\) \(x_1^*(t)\) \(x_2^*(t)\) \(J(x^*(t))\)
1.000 1.000 1.000 2.000
0.100 0.554 0.554 0.614
0.010 0.505 0.505 0.510
0.001 0.501 0.501 0.501

As \(t \to 0\), the barrier solution converges to \((0.5, 0.5)\), confirming that the logarithmic penalty progressively releases the iterates toward the constrained boundary.

The Primal-Dual Interior-Point Method

The basic barrier method described above requires solving a full unconstrained minimisation sub-problem at each outer iteration—an “optimisation within an optimisation.” The primal-dual interior-point method avoids this inefficiency by applying Newton’s method directly to a perturbed version of the KKT system. This is the algorithm used by virtually all modern interior-point solvers, including IPOPT, MOSEK, OOQP, and MATLAB’s quadprog (interior-point-convex option).

The central idea is to introduce slack variables \(s_i = -g_i(x) > 0\) and relax the complementary slackness condition \(\mu_i g_i(x) = 0\) to the perturbed form \(\mu_i s_i = \tau\), where \(\tau > 0\) is the barrier parameter. The complete perturbed KKT system is: \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \nabla J(x) + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \mu_i \nabla g_i(x) + \sum_{j=1}^{q} \lambda_j \nabla h_j(x) &= 0, & & \text{(Stationarity)} \\ h_j(x) &= 0, \quad j = 1,\ldots,q, & & \text{(Equality feasibility)} \\ g_i(x) + s_i &= 0, \quad s_i > 0, \quad i = 1,\ldots,m, & & \text{(Slack definition)} \\ S \mu &= \tau \mathbf{1}, & & \text{(Perturbed complementarity)} \end{aligned} \label{eq:perturbed_kkt} \end{equation}\] where \(S = \operatorname{diag}(s_1, \ldots, s_m)\), \(\mu = (\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_m)^\top\), and \(\mathbf{1}\) is the vector of all ones.

Primal-Dual Interior-Point Method Given the perturbed KKT system [eq:perturbed_kkt], the primal-dual method proceeds as follows:

  1. Linearise: Compute the Newton step \((\Delta x, \Delta s, \Delta \mu, \Delta \lambda)\) by linearising system [eq:perturbed_kkt] around the current iterate \((x_k, s_k, \mu_k, \lambda_k)\).

  2. Step length: Choose \(\alpha \in (0, 1]\) to maintain strict positivity: \(s_k + \alpha \Delta s > 0\) and \(\mu_k + \alpha \Delta \mu > 0\).

  3. Update: Set \((x_{k+1}, s_{k+1}, \mu_{k+1}, \lambda_{k+1}) = (x_k, s_k, \mu_k, \lambda_k) + \alpha (\Delta x, \Delta s, \Delta \mu, \Delta \lambda)\).

  4. Reduce barrier: Update \(\tau \leftarrow \sigma \tau\) with centering parameter \(\sigma \in (0, 1)\).

  5. Repeat until \(\tau < \varepsilon\) and the KKT residual is sufficiently small.

The key advantage over the basic barrier method is efficiency: each iteration requires only one Newton step on the full KKT system, rather than an entire sub-problem solve. Moreover, the Newton system possesses a structured sparsity pattern that can be exploited via block elimination (the Schur complement), reducing the per-iteration linear algebra cost dramatically. Convergence is superlinear near the solution, meaning the method takes fewer total iterations than the basic barrier approach.

Why Primal-Dual Dominates Modern Solvers The primal-dual interior-point method is the algorithm of choice for large-scale optimisation because:

  1. It requires \(O(\sqrt{m}\, \log(1/\varepsilon))\) iterations regardless of the number of variables—each iteration is one Newton step on the KKT system.

  2. It naturally produces both primal (\(x\)) and dual (\(\mu\), \(\lambda\)) solutions, providing sensitivity information for free.

  3. For QPs, the Newton system is a structured linear system with known sparsity, making each iteration very efficient to solve.

Primal-Dual Step: Explicit Construction Consider the same QP from the barrier example: minimise \(J(x) = x_1^2 + x_2^2\) subject to \(x_1 + x_2 \geq 1\), \(x_1 \geq 0\), \(x_2 \geq 0\).

Starting from an interior point \(x^0 = (1,\, 1)^\top\), with slacks \(s^0 = (1,\, 1,\, 1)^\top\), multipliers \(\mu^0 = (1,\, 1,\, 1)^\top\), and barrier parameter \(\tau = 0.1\).

With constraints \(g_1 = 1 - x_1 - x_2\), \(g_2 = -x_1\), \(g_3 = -x_2\), the slacks are \(s_1 = x_1 + x_2 - 1 = 1\), \(s_2 = x_1 = 1\), \(s_3 = x_2 = 1\).

KKT residuals at \((x^0, s^0, \mu^0)\):

Stationarity: \[\nabla J + \mu_1 \nabla g_1 + \mu_2 \nabla g_2 + \mu_3 \nabla g_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \; \checkmark\]

Feasibility: \(g(x^0) + s^0 = 0\). \(\checkmark\)

Perturbed complementarity: \[S\mu - \tau \mathbf{1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot 1 \\ 1 \cdot 1 \\ 1 \cdot 1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0.1 \\ 0.1 \\ 0.1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.9 \\ 0.9 \\ 0.9 \end{bmatrix} \neq 0.\]

The complementarity residual is large because \(\mu_i s_i = 1 \gg \tau = 0.1\). This residual drives the Newton step: the linearised system computes \((\Delta x, \Delta s, \Delta \mu)\) to reduce this gap while maintaining feasibility and stationarity.

After one Newton step with \(\tau = 0.1\), the iterate moves to approximately \(x \approx (0.64,\, 0.64)\). The solver then reduces \(\tau\) and repeats; within a few outer iterations the solution converges to \(x^* = (0.5,\, 0.5)\).

Why This Matters for MPC The numerical methods introduced in this section underpin every MPC implementation:

  • The standard linear MPC problem (Chapters 5–7) is a convex QP. Solvers like quadprog and qpOASES find the global optimum by solving the KKT system—the very conditions derived above.

  • For Nonlinear MPC (Chapter 10), Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) applies Newton’s method to the KKT system of the underlying nonlinear programme, using Armijo-type line searches for globalisation.

  • The primal-dual interior-point method is the backbone of solvers like IPOPT, which solve the nonlinear programmes arising in trajectory optimisation, rocket landing, and autonomous vehicle control.

  • Understanding these fundamentals allows the MPC practitioner to diagnose solver failures (infeasibility, slow convergence, cycling), tune algorithmic parameters (tolerances, barrier parameters), and choose the right solver for each application.

Chapter Summary

This chapter provided the mathematical optimisation foundations required for MPC. The key topics covered are:

  • Problem Formulation: Every optimisation problem consists of decision variables, a cost (objective) function to minimise, and constraints that restrict the feasible set.

  • Convexity: A set is convex if it contains all line segments between its points; a function is convex if its Hessian is positive semi-definite. Convexity guarantees that any local minimum is also the global minimum.

  • Problem Classification: Optimisation problems are classified by the structure of their cost and constraints—Linear Programmes (LP), Quadratic Programmes (QP), Mixed-Integer Linear Programmes (MILP), and Nonlinear Programmes (NLP)—each with different computational properties.

  • Software Tools: Modelling languages such as YALMIP allow the user to specify optimisation problems symbolically; solvers such as quadprog and OSQP then solve the resulting numerical problem.

  • Unconstrained Numerical Methods: Bisection and Newton–Raphson find roots of nonlinear equations; gradient descent iteratively reduces a cost function, with step-size selection governed by the Armijo backtracking condition.

  • Constrained Optimisation Theory: Lagrange multipliers characterise optima under equality constraints. The Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions extend this framework to inequality constraints, providing necessary (and, for convex problems, sufficient) conditions for optimality.

  • Solver Algorithms: Active-set methods enumerate constraint boundaries; interior-point (barrier) methods traverse the interior of the feasible set; primal-dual methods solve the KKT system directly. These algorithms underpin the QP and NLP solvers used in MPC.

Key Takeaways

  • Optimisation is Translation: MPC translates qualitative engineering goals (e.g., “drive fast but don’t crash”) into quantitative mathematical statements (e.g., “minimise travel time subject to velocity limits”).

  • The Scalar Score: An algorithm cannot minimise a vector or a list of conflicting goals. The objective function \(J(x)\) serves to compress complex multi-variable decisions into a single scalar number (a cost), allowing different control strategies to be ranked and compared.

  • Constraints Define Reality: Unlike classical control, where saturation is often an afterthought, optimisation explicitly handles physical limitations. The feasible set is the intersection of all equality and inequality constraints. If this set is empty, the problem is infeasible, and no solution exists.

  • Convexity: The most critical property of an optimisation problem is convexity. If the objective function is convex (bowl-shaped) and the constraints form a convex set, any local minimum found is guaranteed to be the global minimum. This reliability is essential for safety-critical control systems.

  • Standard Forms:

    • LP (Linear Programme): Linear cost and constraints. extremely fast; used for economic dispatch and simple regulation.

    • QP (Quadratic Programme): Quadratic cost (energy/error) and linear constraints. This is the standard formulation for linear MPC.

    • MILP (Mixed-Integer): Contains discrete decisions (On/Off). Computationally expensive but necessary for logic-based control.

  • Minimisation vs. Maximisation: Solvers generally seek to minimise cost. To maximise a benefit (like profit), we simply minimise the negative of that function: \[\arg \max f(x) \equiv \arg \min (-f(x))\]
